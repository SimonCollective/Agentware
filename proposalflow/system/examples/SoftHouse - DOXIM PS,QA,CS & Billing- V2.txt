A note on Quick Wins vs Pilots vs MVPs

In the previous proposal, we suggested building out a set of quick wins
and pilots across the four weeks organically, as we discovered ideal use
cases. This approach allows teams involved to learn the feasibility and
value of these automations. It also provides early knowledge of
challenges, both internal and external.

We define a quick win as anything involving pre-existing, pre-configured
AI technology, i.e:

-   Implementing a better Foundational model for general use

-   Implementing a better meeting note taker that demonstrates
    significant value over existing solutions

-   Implementing a marketing or sales tool that has proven value

-   Implementing automation across engineering or QA that has proven
    value

-   Training for individuals who are using AI in a sub optimal way

We define a pilot as a demonstration of viability using lightweight but
customizable tools, such as IPaaS or Foundational models, an example may
be:
We create a series of three “Custom GPTs”

1)  The first one will take a client request and break it down into a
    series of questions, and clarifications, and it will create a
    structured form, pre-filled with all existing information from the
    request, with spaces for questions and clarifications.

2)  The second one takes the complete completed form and outputs an
    effort assessment, creating a separate document, based on example
    knowledge it contains, this assessment covers both cost, effort, and
    the skill set required

3)  The third one takes the completed form, and the effort assessment,
    and builds out a proposal for the client, and a work order for the
    team.

We generally advise this approach, as it takes significantly less effort
allowing multiple pilots, and gives a broad understanding of the value
and challenges across a range of use cases before significant investment
occurs.

These pilots still leave a large amount of manual process, and whilst
they demo well, in consistent use, their flaws and lack of scalability
start to become apparent. They are designed as fast sense checks,
validations to demonstrate possibility. Yet they are often still used by
the teams, as even in this form they may add significant value.
In this version of the proposal, Doxim have requested that instead of
focusing on breadth of discovery and validation through these POCs /
pilots, we focus on the depth of development of two specific use cases,
alongside quick wins and discovery through workshops and more
traditional discovery. The goal is, rather than a set of pilots, to
build two MVP products that could be in production by the end of the
initial engagement.

This diverts focus to the underlying challenges of “How do we make this
use case work at scale, effectively and efficiently” rather than “what
are the best use cases that are most feasible and add the most value”

For example, the mirror of the above pilot as an MVP would be

1)  We build a vector store of knowledge containing the core processes,
    timelines, and costs of client requests, we ensure we have
    comprehensive AI readable content

2)  We build a frontend chat application leveraging a local or hosted
    LLM that has access to this vector store

3)  We integrate into Salesforce to pull client requests tagged in a
    specific manner.

4)  We create an AI Agent that breaks down the request into a series of
    questions, and clarifications, and is integrated with Salesforce so
    it will ask the client relevant questions

5)  We store the original request and the clarifications in a DB

6)  We create a second AI Agent that accesses this DB and analyses
    effort against data in the Vector DB, and updates the DB with the
    relevant information

7)  We create a third AI agent that Accesses the DB and creates the
    proposal and work order

8)  We then fire all this back into salesforce, attached to the original
    Request.

As you can see, the result is significantly more robust and scalable, an
MVP that can be improved from this point, although the core
functionality is the same as the previous pilot, so whilst it gives us
something to build upon, it does not advance our knowledge of whether
this is feasible or valuable any further than the initial light weight
work.

In terms of effort the pilot would be built in 2 days, whereas this
would take 2 weeks.

This proposal will include the spec and build of 2 MVPs rather than the
original approach of a number of lightweight pilots across all areas.
The initial area of focus will be:

MVP1 - Professional Services - Business Analysts (BA), Improve Process
of Project Document Creation

MVP2 - Data Processing (DP) - Quality Checking in Data Processing

As an aside, I have removed the following from the proposal, as it now runs contrary to the deliverables.  The risk of diving in and building full automations on day one:

70 to 80% of AI projects fail to deliver, often running into problems
with data quality, labelling, and process confidence. Another reason
many fail is employee buy-in, many employees see AI as a threat,
discount its value, or even sabotage attempts to implement. Our approach
offers many benefits.

-   Familiarization: During discovery many people are involved in
    conversations around AI, giving them time to understand the
    potential, and to raise concerns and questions.

-   Assessment: During discovery we get a good understanding of who in
    the organization is an AI advocate, and who may become a blocker.

-   Roadblocks: Detailed discovery allows us to unearth roadblocks that
    may cause a great deal of rework, if discovered our latest stage.

Sustainability: taking the time to understand how to factor maintenance
and further development into Doxim’s core business, removes costly
Reliance on third parties as AI models, update and functionality changes
incredibly quickly, sometimes weekly or monthly.

Building a Sustainable AI Practice at Doxim

6-Week Real-Time Discovery & MVP Implementation Engagement

Introduction & Executive Summary

Doxim operates in the Customer Communications Management (CCM) and
tech-enabled services space, focusing on regulated industries such as
financial services, healthcare, and utilities. The organization has
grown through multiple acquisitions, resulting in 20+ legacy and modern
platforms and a range of manual processes for professional services
(PS), data operations, billing, and client services.

While Doxim has begun exploring AI (e.g., pilots with custom chatbots,
staff using ChatGPT for ad-hoc scripting), there is clear consensus that
manual workflows (particularly in PS, data processing, billing, and
client services) create high costs, lengthy onboarding, and repetitive
tasks that slow the company’s ability to scale profitably. In short,
there is great potential to reduce manual overhead and standardize
processes through targeted AI adoption.

Our proposed engagement is a 6-week, “real-time discovery” effort that
not only maps out Doxim’s detailed pain points and opportunities but
implements quick wins immediately, driving measurable ROI from day one.
By the end of six weeks, Doxim will have a clear AI roadmap, visibility
into immediate cost savings, and tangible prototypes or pilots already
delivering value, all while laying the foundation for a sustainable,
in-house AI practice.

Alongside this discovery we will identify 2 MVP projects and build two
production ready tools to be used by Doxim to drive efficiency into the
Professional Services and Data teams. These MVPs will be developed in
parallel to the discovery process, the technology, and platforms to be
used will be defined in the first two weeks of discovery.

The Doxim Industry & Competitive Landscape

Doxim’s CCM and Document Management solutions sit alongside established
competitors such as Quadient, OpenText, Smart Communications, and others
that already advertise or experiment with generative AI for:

-   Automating form creation and client communications

-   Scaling client onboarding and data mapping

-   Proactive customer support with AI-driven chat and routing

As generative AI has evolved (particularly in the last 2–3 years), many
adjacent CCM or printing & mailing organizations are embedding AI to
reduce staff overhead, accelerate customer onboarding, and create more
dynamic, data-driven forms. Notably:

-   Quadient markets AI-driven document automation.

-   Smart Communications integrates generative AI for template creation
    and interactive client forms.

-   OpenText has made acquisitions that strengthen its AI-based ECM
    (Enterprise Content Management) offerings.

In an industry defined by cost efficiency, turnaround speed, and
regulatory compliance, failing to adopt AI could leave Doxim at a
significant disadvantage. Conversely, quick, high-impact AI adoption can
unlock immediate EBITDA improvements, attractive to private equity
stakeholders and prospective owners in the short to mid-term.

Current Challenges & Pain Points

Over multiple discovery calls with Doxim leadership (CEO, CFO, SVPs of
Professional Services & Client Services, Ops managers), we identified
these key challenges:

1. Professional Services (PS)

-   Large manual effort mapping client data, setting up new clients,
    managing thousands of changes (MACs).

-   Each new implementation is complex: data arrives in unique formats;
    repetitive scripts are hand-built.

2. Data Operations

-   Data QA (pre- and post-processing) is often manual and reactive.

-   Legacy systems lacking robust APIs require humans to fill gaps in
    transformations.

3. Billing

-   ~17 billing staff across inconsistent “middleware” setups.

-   Many manual steps: re-keying data from spreadsheets, from production
    data into GP or other systems.

4. Client Services

-   40 to 50 staff performing day-to-day production support,
    coordinating schedules, raw data checks, insertion requests, and
    small changes.

-   Heavily reliant on Salesforce for tracking but with site-by-site
    variance.

-   Many requests need “human triage” before moving to professional
    services or ops.

Why these challenges persist:

-   Multiple acquisitions led to overlapping platforms, unsynchronized
    processes, and inconsistent automations.

-   Regulatory constraints (banking, healthcare, utilities) discourage
    sending data externally.

-   Standard “one-size-fits-all RPA” approaches from vendors like IBM or
    Fortra failed because the data and processes are too varied.

No single, unified approach to AI initiatives or ongoing AI maintenance;
partial POCs never scaled, inconsistent use of free and paid versions of
ChatGPT across the organization, no clear AI strategy, policy,
governance or roadmap.

Strengths & Rationale for AI

1. Speed & Scalability

Generative AI can interpret varied data formats quickly (including
legacy), automatically mapping data fields and recommending
transformations, drastically reducing staff overhead.

2. Real-Time Discovery & Automation

AI can identify data quality issues up front, highlighting them at a
pre-implementation stage (versus day 12 of a project), saving major
rework.

3. Copilot to Full Automation

Start with a “copilot” approach, AI suggests template code,
transformations, or billing line items. Humans review and correct it.
Over time, the AI “graduates” to full autonomy as trust and training
data grow.

4. Sustainable Practice, Minimizing 3rd-Party Dependency

We upskill your internal teams, ensuring you own your AI practice and
avoid monthly reliance on an outside vendor. AI changes rapidly,
embedding knowledge in-house is essential for staying up-to-date.

Potential Opportunities

Professional Services

1. Automated Data Mapping & Template Generation
A generative AI model could interpret raw client data, identify field
structures, and automatically generate composition scripts/templates.
This accelerates client implementations and slashes repetitive coding.

2. Pre-Implementation Data & QA “Copilot”
Before project kick-off, an AI “copilot” could scan and validate
incoming data for anomalies (e.g., missing control records, incorrect
headers). This preemptive QA shortens overall onboarding timelines and
avoids rework.

3. Automated Scope & Change Estimation
A specialized model could parse client requests, estimate complexity,
and even generate rough “statements of work,” letting PS staff rapidly
quote changes and move them into the pipeline.

Data

1. AI-Driven Data Ingestion & Normalization
A generative AI pipeline could auto-detect data format variations, apply
transformations, and flag outliers, reducing the manual overhead in
normalizing each client’s dataset.

2. Proactive Pre-Processing “Gatekeeper”
An “intelligent gatekeeper” compares incoming files against historical
norms (e.g., typical record length) and alerts staff before they invest
in deeper QA, cutting cycle times.

3. Smart Archive & Retrieval
Beyond simple ECM solutions, an AI engine could classify and index
archived documents, providing intelligent search and automated
compliance tagging, particularly useful for healthcare or finance
audits.

Billing

1. Seamless Middleware Integration
A generative model could automatically pull records from multiple
“middleware” sources, translate them to standardized line items, and
push them into GP, halving the staff overhead spent on repetitive data
entry.

2. Automated Invoice Verification
An AI-driven reconciliation tool could match production logs, shipping
data, and posted statements to verify invoice details or identify
missing charges.

3. Consolidated Billing Insights & Exception Handling
AI could flag anomalies (e.g., outlier invoice amounts) and route
exceptions straight to finance or ops managers, reducing manual triage
and letting staff focus on high-value tasks.

Client Services

1. MAC Request Triage & Auto-Scoping
A chatbot “front end” to parse MAC details (e.g., “What new envelope?
Which design change?”) and auto-create the project skeleton, including
preliminary cost/time estimates, in Salesforce, reducing back-and-forth
by CS staff.

2. Automated Status Updates & Daily Production Reports
A “smart aggregator” could pull daily or weekly status from each ops
location, merge it, and send out templated or even chatbot-driven
updates to clients, cutting staff email volume.

3. Knowledge Base Q&A and Self-Service
Train an internal large language model on Doxim’s process docs, typical
MAC workflows, and historical tickets. Employees or clients ask natural
language questions, and the AI provides step-by-step guidance. This
reduces training time for new hires and cuts routine inquiries from
clients.

Across Professional Services, Data, Billing, and Client Services, we
consistently see duplicated manual effort, site-by-site variance, and
heavy reliance on staff to fill technology gaps. These initial thoughts
represent high-impact scenarios where generative AI or intelligent
automation can drive tangible value for Doxim, and are precisely the
kind of areas that need deep dives to investigate feasibility, ROI, and
implementation path.

Our Proposed 6-Week “Real-Time Discovery” Engagement

Unlike a traditional consulting “assessment” with a single deliverable
in the form of a static report, our Real-Time Discovery is a hands-on,
iterative approach that delivers immediate wins and simultaneously maps
a longer-term roadmap. By the end of the four weeks, you will have:

1. Tangible Quick-Win Implementations

-   As soon as we identify a low-hanging optimization (e.g., streamlined
    billing upload, an AI-based data QA script, or a Mac triage
    improvement), we’ll write it up, and suggest we implement it on the
    spot, no waiting for a final “report.”

2. AI Roadmap & 2 MVPs

-   Full documentation of major opportunities in PS, Data, Billing, and
    Client Services.

-   Technical feasibility outlines, recommended architecture (on-prem
    LLM vs. synthetic data approach for regulated data), timelines, ROI
    for each identified solution.

-   Two use cases will be built out to an initial MVP: Professional
    Services - Business Analysts (BA), Improve Process of Project
    Document Creation and Data Processing (DP) - Quality Checking in
    Data Processing

3. Governance & Best Practices

-   A sustainable AI operating model, including compliance guidelines
    for regulated data, recommended training paths for staff, and
    pilot-to-production scale processes.

4. Financial ROI

-   Clear business-case calculations and KPIs: potential staff reduction
    or capacity reallocation, invoice cycle times, new client throughput
    improvement, etc.
